Quick Reference

Thermal Color Spectrum Guide

HeatTrack's thermal imaging uses a precisely calibrated color spectrum to identify heat stress levels in real time. Each color represents a risk tier: Green for normal conditions, Yellow for early warning, Orange for active intervention, and Red for critical emergencies.

Low Moderate High Critical
Green — Low Risk

Thermal readings are within a safe, normal range. No intervention needed. Continue routine monitoring and maintain current operating conditions.

Yellow — Moderate Risk

Heat levels are rising and approaching concern thresholds. Monitor closely and consider activating preventive cooling measures if conditions continue to climb.

Orange — High Risk

Significant heat stress is present and animals may show behavioral signs. Activate cooling systems immediately and observe animals closely for distress indicators.

Red — Critical Risk

Critical heat stress has been detected and animals are at immediate risk. Emergency intervention is required now. Deploy all available cooling resources and seek veterinary guidance.

Field Guide

Understanding Heat Stress

Learn what heat stress is, what causes it, why early detection matters, and how HeatTrack supports your response.

01

What is heat stress?

Heat stress occurs when a duck's body cannot dissipate heat fast enough and internal temperature rises above safe limits. Early signs are subtle behavioral changes — not just hot air temperatures — which is why thermal imaging is critical for early detection.

  • Open-mouth breathing: a visible sign that cooling is no longer sufficient through normal respiratory mechanisms.
  • Reduced activity: ducks rest more, eat less, and crowd towards the coolest available space.
02

Causes of Heat Stress

Heat stress is driven by more than temperature alone. Humidity, poor airflow, stocking density, and limited water access all interact to determine whether birds can effectively cool themselves down.

  • High humidity: traps heat and significantly reduces the effectiveness of evaporative cooling pathways.
  • Poor ventilation: creates hot pockets and stale air zones where birds struggle most to regulate temperature.
  • Overcrowding: increases collective body heat and reduces the number of cool resting positions available.
03

Why Early Detection Matters

Catching heat stress early gives farm operators a window to act before performance drops, welfare is compromised, or mortality risk increases. Use Live behavior cues, Analytics trends, and Insights alerts together for the fastest and most informed response.

  • Early warning: act before feed intake and growth rates begin to decline — often invisible until it's too late.
  • Data-driven decisions: match thermal readings with barn conditions and behavior to prioritize your response correctly.

Support

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about monitoring, thermal detection, AI guidance, alerts, and flock protection.

Monitoring How does HeatTrack monitor heat stress in real time?

HeatTrack combines live RGB video, infrared thermal imaging, and DHT22 environmental readings. The system tracks temperature, humidity, heat index, and THI level while the AI model flags ducks showing elevated surface temperatures or heat-stress patterns—so you can act before conditions escalate across the flock.

Thermal What is thermal detection and how does it work?

Thermal detection uses an infrared camera to measure surface temperature across each bird. HeatTrack overlays detection boxes on the thermal feed and maps readings to four risk levels—from safe (blue) through high risk (red). Infrared can reveal temperature changes before visible panting or lethargy, giving operators an earlier warning window.

Thermal What do the thermal color spectrum levels mean?

Colors represent relative surface temperature, not exact clinical body temperature. Blue indicates normal warmth; Green is slightly elevated; Yellow suggests moderate heat stress where cooling is recommended; and Red signals high heat stress requiring immediate intervention. Use the spectrum together with behavior cues on the RGB feed.

AI How do AI recommendations work?

When a detection is logged, the Insights view generates context-aware guidance ranked by severity—from Low to Critical. Recommendations cover suggested actions (cooling, relocation, hydration), prevention tips (ventilation, shade, feeding timing), environmental context (humidity and heat index), and temperature follow-up steps. Tap any card in Insights for full detail.

Alerts What notifications and alerts does HeatTrack send?

HeatTrack issues severity-graded alerts (Low, Moderate, High, Critical) when heat stress is detected. Notifications appear in the bell panel, as on-screen toasts, and in the live events stream on Insights. Each alert includes timestamp, severity, and body temperature context so you can prioritize the most urgent cases first.

Alerts Can I control when alerts are delivered?

Yes. In the Insights panel, use System Controls to toggle alert notifications on or off, along with camera streams and the AI detection model. You can also clear the alert history from the bell icon menu. Keep alerts enabled during peak heat hours for the fastest response when conditions change quickly.

Monitoring How often is monitoring data updated?

Camera feeds and thermal frames refresh continuously during active monitoring. DHT22 sensor readings update every few seconds, feeding the live tiles and the 30-reading environmental trend chart. Detection logs and analytics charts reflect events as they are recorded, keeping recommendations aligned with current barn conditions.

Farmers How does HeatTrack help poultry farmers protect their flocks?

HeatTrack gives poultry and duck farmers earlier visibility into heat risk—combining thermal imaging, environmental sensing, and AI guidance in one dashboard. Teams can intervene sooner, reduce mortality risk during heat spikes, export records for management review, and make data-informed decisions instead of relying on observation alone during the hottest shifts.

Farmers Should I still check birds manually if HeatTrack is running?

Absolutely. HeatTrack is an assistive monitoring tool—not a replacement for on-site welfare checks or veterinarian guidance. AI detections and recommendations may contain errors. Always confirm alerts with direct observation and follow your farm protocols before taking critical action, especially for severe or critical severity events.
